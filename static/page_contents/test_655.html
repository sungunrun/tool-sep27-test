Fodor and Pylyshyn’s Conceptions of Modularity;

The Visual System as a Module

In 1983, reacting to New Look psychology, Jerry Fodor offered a vivid conception of
“input systems” as psychological modules. The conception was supposed to provide an
empirical basis for rejecting claims that perception is theory-laden.1060 New Look psych-
ology was already fading when Fodor wrote. His work on modularity summarized and
synthesized a lot of empirical work in the heady early days of computational psychology.
Fodor’s work came, in turn, to influence empirical work. His discussion of modularity is
an important contribution to both psychology and philosophy of psychology. It offered
tools for understanding psychological kinds.

Fodor cites nine marks of modularity.

First, input systems are domain specific. The processing mechanisms are specialized in
having constraints either on the range of information that they can access, or on the range
of distal properties they can represent, or both.1061 Fodor is flexible and largely non-
committal on what to count as specialized processing mechanisms. He allows perceptual
systems that receive inter-modal input to count, for example. He suggests that the process
for color perception, or shape perception, or hybrid systems that function to guide motor
activity through vision, might count as specialized, domain specific systems. These are
clearly overlapping systems.

Second, the operations of the system are mandatory, given relevant input. Fodor cites
non-normal cases in which the condition is not met. He claims, however, a large difference
between perception and thought. Some thought is obsessional. Some proceeds uncon-
sciously, beyond one’s control. But much is under executive control. Often, one can start or
stop thinking, as one pleases. Although one can decide to prevent input into a perceptual
system by closing one’s eyes, one cannot shut off perceptual processing to its natural per-
ceptual outputs, once input occurs.

Third, individuals have only limited access to the mental representations that input sys-
tems compute. Fodor states, as a first approximation, that one is conscious of only the final
output of the system—for example, a conscious visual perception. Representations com-
puted along the way are either inaccessible, or accessible only with considerable difficulty.

Fourth, input systems’ processing is fast. Fodor contrasts the slowness of conscious
thought with fast computations of perceptual systems. He conjectures that computations
by input systems are fast because they are mandatory.1062
