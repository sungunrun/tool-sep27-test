First, Fodor and Pylyshyn wrote at a time when the best vision science took purely per-
ceptual processing to end after early vision, short of categorization, and even short of 3-D
attribution of shapes and spatial relations among them. (See the Chapters 10–11.)
Pylyshyn liberalized the view slightly. He included, in early vision, perceptual representa-
tion of 3-D volume shapes. He still excluded categorization. Fodor and Pylyshyn also
wrote when perceptual processing per se was regarded, again in the best science, as “bot-
tom-up.” They believed that the only non-bottom-up processes that affect perceptual pro-
cessing, while it occurs, are recurrent processes within the main line of perceptual
processing.

Pylyshyn counted anticipation, imagining, and all or nearly all memory as cognitive. He
may have taken attention commands to be cognitive. I reject these positions. They are
connected with Pylyshyn’s failure to distinguish perceptual from conceptual attribution.

Pylyshyn took non-pathological causal influence of cognition on early vision to be
limit ed to two cases: (1) ‘allocations of attention to certain locations or certain properties
prior to the operation of early vision’; and (2) ‘decisions involved in recognizing and iden-
tifying patterns after the operation of early vision’.1139 I am not sure whether Pylyshyn
thought that attention commands are cognitive, or whether he had in mind only cases in
which background beliefs and intentions cause attention. Pylyshyn compared case (1) to
selection of where to point one’s eyes—which clearly does not affect visual processing. He
took attentional or other cognitive effects that occur outside early vision—such as cat egor-
iza tion and object recognition—to be irrelevant to his thesis.

Pylyshyn’s limitation claim is empirically mistaken. Pylyshyn understood early vision
functionally—not in terms of the time of processing, or the anatomical area of processing.
He understood early vision in terms of the types of representations processed. Perceptual
processing of representations as of surface shape and 3-D volume shape, color, orien ta-
tion, size, motion counted for him as early-vision processing. Although influence of
endogenous covert attention occurs after the initial burst that forms first perceptions as of
3-D volume shape and first body-categorizations, endogenous covert attention does affect
perceptual processing of these types of representation.1140 It affects both the quality of
visual representation, and what is represented and how, even in the first 100 ms after
stimu lus onset. (See Chapter 15.) So Pylyshyn was wrong to hold that endogenous atten-
tion does not affect perceptual processing, during processing of 3-D volume shapes. Since
such influences are not cognitive, and since they are computationally integrated with vis-
ual processing, I do not see that Pylyshyn’s mistake matters much.

coveries of multiple forms of feedback. Much feedback affects processing of 3-D volume
