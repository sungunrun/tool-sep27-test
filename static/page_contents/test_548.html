Despite the great speed and frequency at which these unconscious attention shifts
occur, they are not reflexes. They occur in patterns that show the influence of task orienta-
tion, memory of past behavioral success, and selections that obtain the most useful infor-
mation. They are commonly explained representationally—in terms of attention commands,
saccade commands, visual and proprioceptive states themselves, perceptual memory of
perceptual and conative representational states. Such states guide visual search and intra-
saccadic scanning.

In elaborating these remarks, I first sketch some points about the neural underpinnings of
these representational states. Then I discuss computational models of the psychology involved.

Endogenous attention, attention initiations, and saccade initiations have neural support
in the frontal eye fields, FEF, an area in the pre-frontal cortex. Neural activity in FEF sup-
porting attention initiations and attention shifts has been distinguished from neural activ-
ity supporting saccade commands.777 Shifts of attention can occur even if no saccade is
initiated. In fact, the neurons in FEF associated with visual attention (“visual neurons”)
and those associated with motor initiations of saccadic movement (“visuomovement
neuro ns”) have been distinguished.778

FEF receives signals from the retina, at speeds almost as fast as V1 and MT/V5. Some
neurons in FEF respond faster than V1 neurons respond. Every study has found that when
a target is more difficult to locate, neurons in FEF signal its location at least as early and
often earlier than neurons in occipital, parietal, or temporal areas do.779 Most studies have
found that even when a target is easy to locate, attention signals from FEF of the target’s
location are faster than from areas outside the prefrontal cortex.780 This area of the pre-
