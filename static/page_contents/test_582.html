582 Perceptual Memory: Short-Term Systems


Although I have focused on TSM and on issues of perceptual continuity across sac-
cades, there are parallel psychological and neural mechanisms that safeguard perceptual
continuity by compensating for other sorts of bodily movement that would otherwise tend
to make proximal stimulation of the visual system relatively discontinuous.859

It should always be remembered that trans-saccadic neural and psychological processes
are complex. Various mechanisms—neural, sensory-pre-perceptual, visual, and proprio-
ceptive—play roles in producing smooth visual representation across jerky saccades and
other bodily movements that make visual reception decidedly unsmooth. The mech an-
isms of visual stability, visual tracking, visual search, and visual support of motor action
are multi-form and interactive. Given the importance of maintaining steady perceptual
tracking, evolution of psychological systems that do not rely on just one mechanism to
safeguard visual-perceptual functions should be expected.

Let us return to the question of whether TSM is a distinct memory system, or rather an
implementation of the same memory system as visual working memory. The initial com-
parison of TSM with visual working memory depended on considerations of the relative
capacity of each. Using a popular framework of the late 1990s and early 2000s, each was
taken to have a capacity of about 3–4 slots. Slots were taken to correspond to discrete vis-
ual objects. An object’s package of properties—size, shape, color, orientation—was taken
to fit into that object’s slot. I discuss this way of thinking about memory capacity in the
next section. This way of thinking is at best a serious oversimplification. It serves present
purposes. Several experiments did seem to suggest a broadly similar capacity between
TSM and visual working memory. However, many of these experiments suffered the diffi-
culty mentioned earlier. They were not well fitted to conditions in which TSM actually
operates. Many were not ecologically valid.

More recent evidence suggests that the storage capacity of TSM may have a lower bound
that is roughly quadruple the common rough estimate of the capacity of visual working
memory.860 If this result holds up, it would suggest that TSM is a distinct system from
both iconic memory (which has a vastly larger capacity) and working memory. Because
the evidence is subject to various confounds and has not been developed or replicated, the
issue remains open.
