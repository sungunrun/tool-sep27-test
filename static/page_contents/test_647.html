Perception and Cognition

Encoded in the large, highly evolved sensory and motor portions of the

human brain is a billion years of experience about the nature of the world and

how to survive in it. . . . We are all prodigious Olympians in perceptual and

motor areas, so good that we make the difficult look easy. Abstract thought,

though, is a new trick, perhaps less than 100 thousand years old. We have not

yet mastered it. It is not all that intrinsically difficult; it just seems so when

we do it.

Hans Moravec, Mind Children: The Future of Robot and Human Intelligence

In Chapters 10–11, I sketched core perceptual processing. I emphasized that first forma-
tion of perceptual states is very fast. I stressed that first-formed perceptual states do not
depend on occurrent input from attention or memory. I emphasized that first-formed per-
ception extends to categorization. In Chapter 11, I discussed recurrent processing and
input from endogenous attention and memory into production of perceptual states,
micro-seconds after the first perceptual states are produced. In Chapter 12, I elaborated
two cases of probable perceptual attributives—cause and act—that are late, both in the
order perception-formation and in the evolution of perceptual systems. I speculated on
the existence of perceptual functional attributives.

Chapters 13–18 centered on categorization and on capacities other than perception that
are natural companions of perception—attention, conation, affect, memory, learning,
anticipation, and imagining. I emphasized that each of these generic capacities has a
perceptual-level species—a species that uses only perceptual attributives and that has
operations that are not more advanced than those in perception-formation. Although it is
not certain that conation uses only perceptual attributives, I conjectured that all attribu-
tives in these associated, perceptual-level capacities are borrowed from one or another
perceptual modality. I conjectured that representational conation adds only command
mode, not attributives not already provided by perception. Even if this conjecture is mis-
taken, there seems to be a level of explanation at which all motor attributives are embed-
ded in operations not more sophisticated than those that occur in the perceptual
modalities. With few exceptions (possibly episodic memory and endogenously controlled
imagining), all the companion capacities evolved in conjunction with, not later than, per-
ceptual systems.

A central point of this work in Chapters 10–18 is to lay the groundwork for two themes.
