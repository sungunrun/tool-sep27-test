424 Intra-Saccadic Perception and Recurrent Processing


A constant issue in scientific discussions of perceptual categorization is whether an
abstract category, like animal-body, is really applied, or whether only a grouping of lower-
level properties (“features”), such as combinations of color, texture, types of movement,
and shapes, is applied. The lower-level properties usually occur when the category-
instance occurs.

The main perceptual categorizations must be cued by attributions of some lower-level
properties. One cannot just see something as a body or a face without using shape, size,
motion, texture, or color cues. Lower-level attributions of shape and size are made when-
ever categorizing attributions of body or face are made. The former comprise the applica-
tion base of the latter. Attributives are representational capacities. If one is to be justified in
taking a perceiver to engage in categorization, one must find some capacity beyond that of
attributing the application base.

Scientists are acutely aware of the issue. Experiments often aim to rule out reduction of
a proposed categorization to lower-level attributions. Sometimes, experiments are fash-
ioned to deflate a proposed categorization in favor of attribution of lower-level features.
I believe that many experiments in each direction are not rigorous enough to be con vin-
cing. Still, I think that it is impossible to undermine all experiments that postulate percep-
tual categorization, by postulating only perceptual attributions of lower-level features.
Attributions of body are distinguished from attributions of lower-level features by showing
that there is a disposition to anticipate certain temporal continuities. Other cat egor iza-
tions apply to sub-classes of bodies, distinguished by characteristic shape.526 Primate cat-
egor iza tion is prominently supported in the infero-temporal cortex (IT), though it is also
supported by other brain regions, temporal and otherwise.527

Let us return to Thorpe’s discovery of ultra-rapid categorization. Humans can visually
detect whether or not something is an animal-body in circa 90 ms after the onset of a very
brief stimulus exposure. Detection of faces is slightly faster, circa 80 ms. Detection of
reptile-bodies occurs in 70 ms.

These results are for an initial, dominantly feed-forward process. Feedback from the
parietal, orbital-pre-frontal and pre-frontal areas figures in later, consolidation stages.
Arrival of such feedback comes by 140–180 ms.528 Such feedback often reinforces the ini-
tial response. I know of no evidence that even this feedback initially provides information
sufficiently detailed to recognize the same object again, though for very familiar objects
