Analogous points apply for touch. Normally, one does not hear a sound without hearing

Third, numerous relational attributives are induced by spatial aspects of most percep-
tual representations. The fact that one body representation bears a geometric relation to
another body representation in an iconic format induces not only the to-the-left-of attribu-
tive. It also induces many other distance and directional relations, and situates each body
in relation to other entities.

Fourth, the role of temporal representational frameworks in perception induces further
complexity in a perception, and in perceptual memories and perceptual anticipations. In
representing an event, perception represents temporal relations between that event
and others.

In sum, in perception, a multitude of attributions and referential applications occur in
the representational content any given perception. I have identified four sources of com-
plexity: (a) multiple levels of abstraction under most generic of perceptual attributives
(attributives for shape, color, lightness, edge, surface, and body, for example); (b) pack-
aging different types of attributives; (c) the systematic framework aspects of spatial repre-
sentation; and (d) the systematic framework aspects of temporal representation. There are
no doubt further sources.

Complexity in perceptual representation is not a consequence of iconicity per se.
Certain diagrams are iconic and lack these types of complexity. A digitally marked chart
that represents population by a bar’s height would represent only a specific number of
people. Such iconicity abstracts to a single property. Representation of color shades by
same-colored color-shaded chits also exemplifies iconic representation that does not
represent packages of properties.

What makes iconic packaging of properties—and other iconic representation—com-
mon in natural systems of perceptual representation is that the evolution of such systems
molded them to the natural world. Surfaces, bodies, shapes, sizes, colors, textures come as
packages in the natural world. Sensory systems evolved to represent at least as much about
the world as is useful for the organism, given the complexity and sensitivity of the organ-
ism’s equipment. Psychological systems that do not represent attributes in packages are
either extremely primitive, or involve conventional abstraction that is useful for commu-
nication or analytical control.
