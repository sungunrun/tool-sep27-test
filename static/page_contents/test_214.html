Both sameness of what is represented (the attribute) and difference in how the attribute
is presented are coded in the perceptual system. Different perceptual-state-types are com-
mitted to a single given attribute. The different attributives exercised in a perceptual con-
stancy code (by indicating and attributing) the given attribute. The sameness of attribute
in the different attributives is coded in the semantics of the different attributives, not in any
one state or representational content.

To make the point concrete, let us return to the edge-length example. Each of the per-
ceptual states that responds to the edge at different slants represents the edge as being an
edge and as having length l. Each attributive in the different states indicates and attributes
the kind edge or the property length l in the different stimulus conditions. The attributives
are different, just as the representational contents of the terms are different in Frege’s (a-b)
cases. Unlike Frege’s (a-b) cases, the sameness of representata is coded into the content of
the attributives. The constancy that centers on length codes the sameness in the different
length attributives.

This is a difference between perception and Frege’s case. Although a and b in fact have
the same referent, that sameness is not coded into the representational content of a or b.
There is nothing in the representational contents the morning star and the evening
star that commits to their referring to the same entity. One could use the terms or the
representational contents competently and never have a commitment on whether they
share a referent. I discuss this difference in this chapter, the section Perceptual Units
in Packages in Iconic Visual Spatial Representation.

Minimalism: Defocus and Color Constancy

So far I have supported the view:

(I) If a perceptual constancy centers on an attribute, then distinct exercises of the

constancy, prompted by differing proximal inputs, commonly yield perceptual states

featuring distinct perceptual attributives that represent the same physical attribute.

Let us pursue our edge-length example. Different perceptions as of an edge derive from
the different stimulus conditions. They derive from perceiving an edge at different orien ta-
tions or distances. (I) holds that different perceptual attributives occur in the different
perceptual states.
