Primitive Attribution of Mechanical Causation 447


Unlike primitive representation of most spatio-temporal relations,569 primitive repre-
sentation of causation—representation of mechanical causation—seems not to be egocen-
trically indexed. It does not implicate the representer’s perspective in indicating the
relation. What marks causal representation as a step toward the objectivity prized in sci-
ence is not just attribution of a relation per se, but attribution of a relation not indexed to
the representer. The world is represented as hanging together, without reference to the
representer’s position or perspective in it.

There is a class of representations of causal relations that is structurally different. I mean
representation of causal relations between the representer’s own act, marked in some ego-
centric way, and an effect of the act. Many have thought that this type of representation is
the developmental and phylogenetic root of all representation of causation. The idea is
that one learns about causation from one’s own activity on the world. As far as I know, this
idea has not been shown empirically correct, although the issue is still open. Self-causation
is attributed at latest by 4½ months. Mechanical causation is attributed by infants at 3
months.570

Hume and Kant claimed that perception cannot represent causation. Hume held that, in
collisions, all that we perceive (or attribute) is one body moving into contiguity with a
second body and the second body moving away from the first right after being spatially
contiguous with it:

When we consider these objects with the utmost attention, we find only that the one body

approaches the other; and the motion of it precedes that of the other without any sensible

interval.571

Motion in the second billiard ball, hit by the first, is a quite distinct event from motion in

the first. Nor is there any thing in the one to suggest the smallest hint of the other.572
