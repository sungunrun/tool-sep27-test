188 Form and Semantics of Perceptual Contents


Our visual abilities both to categorize edges and surfaces and to relate them to other
specific entities that they are seen as edges of and surfaces of suggests postulation of pairs
of nominal and relational attributives: edge and edge-of, surface and surface of.

Vision can pick out instances of cubical. It can characterize them in intra-attribute attri-
butions. It can also attribute the instances and the attribute cubical to instances of other
attributes in cross-attribute attribution. The question which instance of cubical a body is
seen to have does not arise. There can be only one instance of cubical in the whole body.
Pressure for a relational attribution is absent.

Color attributives may differ from shape attributives in that a given color-instance can
be present in different perceptually characterized entities. The same color-instance can be
seen as being in a body and also in the body’s overall surface.191

The visual system does not attribute many-one relations between shape or color-
instances, on one hand, and instances of entities that they are shapes or colors of, on
the other.

Assume that an individual attends to the shape of a body, not the body that it is a shape
of. Let us compare this case to the case discussed earlier, in which the system focuses on
the surface of a body, not the body that it is a surface of. To repeat an abbreviated form of
the latter case:

(that x1)[(that x2)(body(i)(x2), surface-of(c)(x1, x2), surface(i)(x1))].

The form for the case in which the shape is categorized and attended to is:

(that x1)[(that x2)(body(i)(x2), cubical(c)(x2), cubical(i)(x1))].

There is only one instance of cubical in the whole body. There is no occasion for a visual
system to represent there being more. So the question of which instance characterizes the
body does not arise. So there is no need for a relational cubical attributive.

Although the point about shape attributives is partly grounded in ontology, it depends
also on a conjecture about perceptual competence. Sometimes perceptual capacities do
not line up with ontology. I conjecture, however, that not only is it ontologically im pos-
sible for there to be two instances of a given shape for a whole single body at a given time,
but also that the visual system cannot represent there being two. The system could prod-
uce a double image of shape on what is perceived to be a single body. I doubt, however,
that the case is best construed as the visual system’s presenting the same body as having
two instances of the shape in the body.

Let us shift from discussion of the form of shape, surface, and edge attributives, to the
attributive body. Referential applications governing body are usually scope-dominant. For
us, dealing with bodies is commonly most important, ecologically, for survival. In certain
contexts, as noted, they can be less psychologically central, for attention, action, or mem-
ory than surface attributives, or shape attributives, or color attributives, or even edge
attributives. Does body differ from the other types of attributive in some more funda-
mental way?
