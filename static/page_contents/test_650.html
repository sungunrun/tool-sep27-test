In the 1950s some psychologists made parallel claims about perception’s requiring con-
cepts, with different motivations. New Look psychologists held that perception sys tem at ic-
al ly depends on concepts.1046 The view remained influential into the 1970s. Then, as now,
the term ‘concept’ was used in various ways, usually without a precise meaning. New Look
psychology seemed to imply, however, that perception is inevitably theory-laden. So it
implied that perception depends on input from concepts, in my sense of ‘concept’—com-
ponents of propositional structures.

The philosophers whose views paralleled New Look psychology held them as part of a
theory of necessary conditions for representation, or intentionality, or reference. Although
some found New Look psychology congenial, few were interested enough in psychology
to be influenced by it.

Another group of philosophers paid more attention to New Look Psychology and used
it.1047 They took it to support doubts about the objectivity of science. The primary impetus
for their doubts stemmed from their theories of meaning.

As part of his critique of positivism, W.V. Quine espoused meaning holism. On this
view, units of empirical meaning are not words or sentences, but systems of sentences—
theories. Quine questioned a distinction between differences in belief and differences in
meaning.

Several philosophers inferred from less radical analogs of Quine’s meaning holism that
there is no objective way to decide between scientific theories that differ in basic the or et-
ic al commitments. The idea was that the linguistic meaning of scientific statements
depends on, and only on, the inferences that they figure in. For Quine, all inferences
