Many controversies over mental imagery have been settled empirically. I think it no
longer reasonable to doubt that imagery occurs in mental life, or that imagery discussed in
empirical work has an iconic format derivative from perception.1026 No one thinks that
imagery must always be consciously pictorial. No one thinks that images, like pictures, are
internally “looked at”.1027

Although the format and representational content of imagery derive from perception,
imagining and perception are dissociable. Some brain-damaged patients who cannot gen-
erate images can respond to perception. Some who can generate images have serious per-
ceptual deficits. There is evidence that the capacity for imagining is carried out by joint
operation of several processes.1028 Of course, imagining can occur without current input.
Some uses of visual imaging do not engage lower visual areas (such as V1). Some do. One
can perceive without using memory. Perceptual imagining requires some reliance on
remembered perceptual contents.1029

However, there is evidence that imagining and perception share some of the same brain
areas, sometimes specialized to the specific entities that are imagined or perceived. For
example, both perception and imagining of faces are supported in the fusiform face area.
Both perception and imagining of places, or scene-types, are supported in the hippocam-
pal complex.1030

Not all visual imagery is driven by manipulating visual-perceptual capacities. Some
solutions to mental rotation problems may be driven by imagining what one would see if
one rotated the object, as contrasted with what one would see if the object rotated via
some outside force. In some responses to mental-rotation-type experiments, subjects’ sen-
sorimotor systems are strongly activated. In other cases, not.1031 I focus on the latter type.
What I have to say about imagery’s relation to perception applies to the former type, with
qualifications.

A key result in the initial Shepard and Metzler experiments and in subsequent ex peri-
ments of the same type is that the time it takes subjects to answer a question about whether
two images (2-D or 3-D) that are rotated (either in a plane or in depth) have the same
shape is proportional to the degree of angle of rotation between the figures.1032
