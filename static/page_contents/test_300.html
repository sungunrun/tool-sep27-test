Iconicity can occur with respect to higher-order as well as first-order representational
properties. A history book represents its subject matter through conventional linguistic
symbols. The spatial order of the narrative may map iconically into the temporal order of
the events.301

So far, I have explicated iconic representation. Iconicity is not a property purely of repre-
sentations and representational contents. It is also a property of non-perceptual, sensory
information registrations. A parallel explication applies to iconic non-perceptual sensory
information registration. If a simple organism registers the day–night time cycle via a state
that is excited during the day and a state that is quiet at night, each of the state-types icon-
ic al ly registers segments of the diurnal time cycle. The 1–1 mapping of sensory states into
day and night is natural and systematic. It would preserve the cyclic temporal relations
between day and night. By contrast, a monkey’s danger call in response to a hawk may
have no iconic element. It may not map into the registered physical facts in a way that
preserves natural relations among those facts.

A non-perceptual, non-representational sensory information registration state is iconic
by virtue of meeting the following conditions:

(1΄) There is a natural, systematic 1–1-into mapping from one or more aspects of the

sensory registration state and corresponding aspects of a domain that the sensory

state functions to register.

(2΄) The mapping in (1΄) preserves correlations between some relations among struc-

tural elements of the sensory registration state and natural relations among elements

in the registered domain.

(3΄) The sensory registration state registers the relevant elements and their relations

partly by way of the mapping of (1) and (2).

The central notion in the explications (1)–(3) and (1΄)–(3΄) is that of being natural.
