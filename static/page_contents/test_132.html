and

(that x1)(that x2)[F(x1)G(x2)H(x1, x2)]

are common.

For example, a perception (or aspect of a perception) that represents an instance of a
color shade of red would have a form read like: (that x1)(red(x1)). A perception of one
body as being larger than another would have a form read like:

(that x1)(that x2)(body(x1)body(x2)L(x1, x2))

—that x1 body, that x2 body, x1 being larger than x2.

These forms are known via reflection on the representational function and representa-
tional capacities of perception. The representational function of perception is to pick out
particulars. Perception picks out particulars through its occurrences’ picking them out
and characterizing them. Two points bear emphasis. First, there is always at least one
context-bound referential application with widest scope. Second, every context-bound
referential application is also an occurrent attributional application of characterizing
elem ents—perceptual attributives. Thus the dominant picking-out function is always
aided by the characterizing function. Perception is never bare or neat. It is always percep-
tion of something as such and such or thus and so.

These points are in direct opposition to the idea, occasionally put forward, that percep-
tion sometimes picks out a particular without any perceptual characterizing of the
particular.138

I think this a wildly mistaken position. I think it both conceptually incoherent and
without empirical support. I think that it is apriori that it is impossible to perceive some-
thing without perceiving it as being some way.

What we know from empirical science is also incompatible with the mistaken position.
The primary job of perceptual psychology is to explain formations of attributives—exercises
of repeatable perceptual capacities that indicate properties and relations—that figure in
successful or unsuccessful perception. There is no evidence from the practice of the sci-
ence that perception ever occurs in the absence of such attributives. I elaborate these
points in what follows.139
