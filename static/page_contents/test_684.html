This third issue is specifying what counts as a cognitive penetrator and what counts as a
perceptual or perceptual-level system that may be vulnerable to penetration. Here I think that
both scientific and philosophical literatures are a royal mess. Many specialized discussions
circumvent it by focusing on belief or propositional-level intention as candidate penetrators.
Some of these discussions have been sensible. A more general understanding is needed.

A fourth issue is specifying the causal relation between cognition and perception that is
to count as penetration. (MP) takes as cognitive penetration any causal influence of cogni-
tion on visual processing. MacPherson separately adds that the causation must not be
non-standard. She writes that the causal links must make the content of the perceptual
experience intelligible, or must be ‘in some very minimal sense rational’, in light of the
content of the cognitive state.

This rider makes a pass at according with Pylyshyn’s nebulously formulated restrictions
on causation. His vague talk of logical connection and semantic coherence plausibly
requires that the connection must not merely be de facto semantically coherent. It must be
coherent based in analogs of structurally good argument. The psychology must make the
connection, not merely have it fall out of a happen-so event. Suppose that one believes that
there will be an explosion with bright lights. Suppose that this belief causes a wave of anx-
iety that produces an illusion of light flashes of the very sort that one might see if there
were an explosion. Such a cause does not count as semantically coherent and logically
connected to the perceptual state. The belief causes the perceptual state. There is a de facto
“semantic coherence” between the two. No one would think that this is cognitive pene tra-
tion. It is pathological causation. Normally, cognitive penetration must be subject to nor-
mal causal explanation of a non-pathological occurrent perceptual (or perceptual-level)
state, and its content, in terms of a cognitive state, and its content.

Most work on issues of cognitive penetration says little more than I just have. I think
that Pylyshyn and Fodor were trying to get at something that their own formulations do
not capture. I develop this point shortly. Of course, my main interest is not interpreting or
amplifying their views. It is understanding a notion of penetration that accords better than
do most current construals with the practice and scientific understanding of perceptual
and motor psychology.

Conceptions of Penetration

In this section, I begin to develop a sharper understanding of penetration. Pylyshyn writes:

The sort of influence that concerns us here originates outside the visual system and affects

the content of visual perception (what is seen) in a certain meaning-dependent way that we

call cognitive penetration . . . if a system is cognitively penetrable then the function it com-

putes is sensitive, in a semantically coherent way, to the organism’s goals and beliefs, that is,

it can be altered in a way that bears some logical relation to what the person knows.1122
