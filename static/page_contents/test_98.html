Neural analogs of this rough sequence occur in the ventral processing sequence, start-
ing with the first cortical area specialized for vision, V1, proceeding through V2 and V4,
and culminating with categorization on the basis of shape in the inferior temporal cortex
(IT). Although this ventral sequence is by no means the only neural underpinning of spa-
tial perceptual constancies, it is the most studied one.

The first spatial perceptual constancies may be supported in first-pass V4—that is, just
after signals from retinal sensors first get to V4, before recurrent processing or “top-down”
feedback. V4 seems to support rudimentary constancies for 3-D volume shape and size.
The most rudimentary 3-D spatial constancies are formed, supported by V4, by 58 milli-
seconds (58 ms) from stimulus onset.103 Perceptual representation of motion in depth may
already be supported in MT, an area that gets some retinal signals even faster than V1 does.

The most generic categorization of bodies, by their having a 3-D volume shape with
closed outer boundary, may also be formed by that time, supported in V4.

By the time proximal stimuli generate relevant neural responses in IT—in roughly
70–80 ms from stimulus onset—some of the basic spatial perceptual constancies and the
most elementary categorization that is based on attributions of specific spatial shape-
properties are in operation. For example, by then, categorization of an object that is
grouped with animal-bodies occurs. Categorizations of faces occur by then.104 These are
first-pass, sketch-like perceptual categorizations. These categorizations, like the spatial
attributions on which they are based, are perceptual—associated with perceptual constan-
cies. (See Chapter 11, the section Categorization and TiMing.)

As noted in Chapter 1, there is a wave of feedback after this first pass of perception-
formation, beginning roughly 125–140 ms after stimulus onset. The feedback is from
higher to lower visual areas and from perceptual memories. This wave has the effect of
filling in the roughest spatial and object representations that had already been produced.
