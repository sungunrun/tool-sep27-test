Of course, each proposal must be evaluated on its merits. In view of the poor track
record of claims that the visual system is stocked with a great variety of categories of rela-
tively sophisticated attributives—caution is in order.

Expertise enables experts to perceive differently. It is easy, but mistaken, just to infer
that experts must have sophisticated perceptual attributives for the kinds that mark the
topic of their expertise: natural kinds of birds for bird watchers, types of cancer for readers
of x-ray machines, types of antique pianos for music historians, and so on.

Acquiring recognitional expertise consists, largely, in attending to low-level attributes
and grouping them in new ways, through perceptual learning, and remembering them
better.552 Familiarity affects organization of perceptual capacities for grouping shapes.553
Expertise-guided attention to specific shapes can help form learned groupings of shape
attributives that provide a refined perceptual basis for applying non-perceptual attribu-
tives. New spatial attributives may be acquired via similarity groupings. No new range of
perceptual attributives (such as a natural-kind range for birds) need be acquired. Attention
and grouping can depend on expert knowledge. Such knowledge does not introduce
theory-laden perceptual attributives into the perceptual system.

For example, acquiring expertise in spotting bird species can develop a capacity to
attend to, group, and remember certain shapes, in ways that a non-expert would not. An
ornithologist can group a range of body-shapes and beak-shapes to sharpen application of
a bird-species concept. The novice sees the same body-shapes and beak shapes, but does
not attend to similarities and differences. The novice does not group or remember them as
the expert does. Such differences can motivate saying that the expert sees the world differ-
ently. They do not motivate claiming that the expert has perceptual attributives as of bird
species that the novice lacks.

A second standard scientific approach to explaining cases that mistakenly yield assump-
tion of sophisticated attributives in perception is complimentary to the first. This strategy
postulates non-perceptual learning, in memory, of conceptual attributives. Applications of
these conceptual attributives to instances relies on perception that uses less sophisticated
attributions—for example, shape attributions—in perception.554, 555

Claims that perception includes sophisticated perceptual attributives, commonly just
assume that if an attributive is applied quickly and automatically on the basis of percep-
tion, it is perceptual. Experiment and theory must determine whether an attributive is
formed in a perceptual system, or is instead formed via processes operating partly on non-
perceptual aspects of long-term memory.
