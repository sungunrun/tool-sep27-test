Second, the system must produce a representation of the difference between the pos-
ition of the hand and the position of the target. Since the visual system is dominant in
mapping the action, the specification of hand position that derives from proprioception
and other perceptual sources must be translated into a specification of the hand’s position
within the spatial coordinate system with a visual anchor, say the cyclopean eye, the mid-
way point between the two eyes.694

Third, the system must issue a motor command that gets the hand to follow a trajectory
that begins with its initial position and ends at the target. This stage involves computation
of a difference vector, a straight-line involving distance and direction from hand position
to target.695 Both the difference vector and the target-object’s position—the end position
of the movement—are represented in a motor command.696 The difference vector is the
basis for the motor command. The motor command could command traversal of the vec-
tor in many ways, because there are many degrees of freedom for getting the hand to move
by moving the arm. Which of these ways is caused depends on several cost-benefit factors,
to which I shall return.

Much of the experimentation on the structure and content of the psychology of rela-
tively primitive action centers on perturbations of relations between key psychological
subsystems. For example, perception of the results of an action might be distorted by
channeling perception through a prism. The agent’s response over time is studied. Or per-
ception might be left undisturbed, but a perturbation on the motor movement might be
introduced. A normal movement of a hand might be biased so as to leave the hand (or an
effector, such as a cursor, controlled by the hand) at some remove from where the same
initiating movement would ordinarily take it. Or some distortion of the relation between
different perceptual modalities might be introduced. Adaptation to such abnormal cir-
cumstances illumines the capabilities involved in normal action. Adaptation to normal
perturbations is also a central topic of study. Noise in the psychological system is a nor-
mal, indeed inevitable, perturbation. Fatigue and aging are other normal perturbations
that require adaptation of relations among perceptual, conative, retentive, and anticipa-
tory states. Limitations on space and on my own knowledge prevent a survey of the enor-
mously complex literature.
