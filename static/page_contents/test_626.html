I also follow standard explications in counting relatively stable pre-perceptual sensory
changes that derive from sensory stimulation as perceptual learning, even though the
changes are not perceptual. To count as perceptual learning, such changes must occur in a
perceptual system and affect perceptual processing. This liberality in explication is, of
course, compatible with distinguishing between non-perceptual sensory registration and
perception.

Pre-perceptual changes in acquisition of sensory information have been found in detec-
tion of differences in spatial frequency, orientation, lightness signals, and scribbles. Some
studies show that individuals can learn to make discriminations that are finer than the
spacing between photo-receptors. Then, the sensory basis for discrimination does not
depend on formation of perceptions or being embedded in perceptual constancies.981
Such changes do not transfer across orientations, retinal positions, distances. Sometimes
there is just improvement in discriminability.

Such perceptual learning usually depends on task-oriented attention. Feedback regard-
ing success or failure is not required, but it tends to speed learning. In classically powerful
work, some low-level types of perceptual learning result almost entirely from selective re-
weighting of processing of inputs and reduction of noise—classical effects of applications
of attention.982
