When one is told the length by ‘this length’, one relies on more than the words literally
and conventionally express. In addition to the singular application of ‘this’ and the at tribu-
tive length, one relies on a perceptual attributive that indicates the specific length. This
indicator is not perspective-free, as the actual length of the stick is (apart from Einsteinian
relativity), and as the specification ‘one meter long’ is. The perceptual attributive indicates
the specific length in a way that plots its position—and those of its discernible parts—in
an egocentrically anchored coordinate system. The basic perceptual specification of the
length uses, essentially, perspective-dependent plottings.

Many conceptual systems and nearly all linguistic systems use canonical, amodal,
perspective-free specifications of length and shape—2 meters and ‘square’. Some sub-
propositional, supra-perceptual-level psychological systems may use unit-free specifica-
tions of magnitudes. There are modal, object-centered specifications of spatial properties
in perceptual systems. I think that the basic attributives for length and shape in perceptual
systems are unit-free, modal, and perspectival. They consist in plottings in an ego cen tric-
al ly anchored perceptual spatial coordinate system.

The two illustrations that I have provided support rejecting minimalism and maintain-
ing that basic perceptual attributives are individuated more finely than the indicants of
those attributives. There are many basic perceptual attributives—many different percep-
tual ways of indicating and attributing any given indicant. Each attributive is a different
perceptual capacity with different psychological properties. The considerations so far
given have highlighted non-relational, “intrinsic”, perceiver-independent, spatial proper-
ties of environmental particulars that are perceptually attributed. The considerations high-
lighted non-relational properties like square, cubical, straight, of length l, of size s. I
employed this emphasis because rejecting minimalism is most easily made intuitive by
centering on representations of these types of properties. The illustrations suggest the
huge variety of attributives in any perceptual system for each non-relational spatial prop-
erty. I have, however, explained that the anti-minimalist point applies equally to percep-
tual representation of spatial relations, such as x farther than y from ego-here, x at distance
d from ego-here, x in orientation o with respect to ego-here, x at distance d8 from y. For any
given environmental spatial relation that is perceptually attributable in any given percep-
tual system, there are many basic relational attributives that can attribute that relation (or
“relational property”).

In order to bring out this point more fully, I summarize and develop rejection of min-
im al ism for perceptual spatial attributions in a more general way. Understanding why
minimalism is mistaken here goes close to the heart of understanding perceptual repre-
sentation, with its fundamentally iconic format for realizing perceptual constancies.

Every normal perceptual state is an enormous complex of referential applications and
perceptual attributions. Any occurrent perceptual state is caused by a specific occurrent
proximal stimulus. The occurrence of the perceptual state is a joint occurrent exercise of
an enormous number of sub-capacities. These sub-capacities are all species of the picking
out capacity—referential applications—or the species of the characterizing capacities—the
attributives. Each attributive is a capacity to indicate and attribute a property (which here,
comprises kinds as well as features) or a relation. Numerous attributives are jointly exer-
cised in any normal, full, occurrent perceptual state.
