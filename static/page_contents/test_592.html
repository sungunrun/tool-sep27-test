592 Perceptual Memory: Short-Term Systems


It is widely believed that the storage capacity of working memory is small. However,
there has been little study of its encoding limits. Unless encoding limits are well under-
stood, one cannot be certain what part of the limited recall in visual working memory is
due to storage limits and what part is due to encoding limits. Nearby items, or similar
items presented simultaneously, can be in competition for encoding into storage. Encoding
can also depend on position in the visual field and item category. Configuration in a dis-
play can affect what is encoded about particular items.878

I assume with most of the field, however, that a significant factor in limiting visual
working memory is storage capacity. Working memory seems to have a smaller storage
capacity than FVSTM. It has a much smaller storage capacity than visual sensory memory,
whose capacity is comparable to that of the original perception, and long-term memory that
is not easily accessible. In the easily consciously accessible aspects of visual memory, work-
ing memory is a bottleneck.

The question of the storage or load capacity of working memory has engendered one of
the most intense disputes in the field.

The first view to be proposed was that storage capacity should be understood in terms
of slots for items of information, with fixed resolution. It was proposed that for adult
humans, working memory has 3-4 slots. Defenders usually took a slot to hold a represen-
tation of an integrated object with a variable number of features. Adding features, in cross-
attribute attribution, to an object was supposed not to add to the load, as long as they were
associated with that object. This view was inspired by George Miller’s famous invocation
of a magic number 7.879 After some years, an opposing view emerged. It claimed that there
is a total energy capacity that can be distributed over a varying number of stored items,
with varying resolution.880

As with most issues in science that are so simply explained, this one turned out to be
much more complex than initial proposals on each side maintained. Evidence of trade-
offs between quantity of information and quality of resolution seemed to undermine slot
proposals.881 More objects could be remembered at lower resolution, or if only a small
