Leibniz postulated a distinction between discrimination and identification. I take his iden-
tification to be approximately equivalent to what I have been calling ‘categorization’. An
“idea”, including a perceptual representation, that merely discriminates something from a
surround was termed ‘obscure’. An idea that effected identification of a type or attribute
was termed ‘clear’.488

On a rough, initial gloss, the result just discussed can be characterized in this way: For
first-formed perceptual states, as soon as one forms a representation that discriminates
something from a surround, one often has a clear representation.

Standard models for ultra-rapid, first categorizations are dominantly feed-forward—
from initial registration of proximal stimulus to first formation of a perceptual
categorization.

The idea of a predominantly feed-forward, pre-attentive categorization process provides
a rough picture of what goes on. There are, however, loops even within early low-level
processes, where, for example, the results of a second stage of processing are fed back to a
first stage. Such loops enrich and improve the work of the first stage. The point is not that
there is no feedback during the fastest processing. The point is that feedback needed to
produce first perceptual representations does not require infusions from attention, or
occurrent input from memory, much less infusions from cognitive or conceptual repre-
sentation.489 Attention is sometimes needed to resolve crowding or to handle cases in
which cues are impoverished. The kinds of feedback necessary to resolve special problems
vary with the case.

Resolution is best in foveated areas. Normally, attention is fixed in these areas. Outside
them, resolution falls off rapidly. Perceptual representation nevertheless occurs across the
whole visual field, out to the extreme periphery. Within this range, including both foveal
areas and pre-attentive extra-foveal areas, perceptual representation as of a limited num-
ber of objects, including bodies, in natural scenes is processed in parallel. Perceptual repre-
sentation is organized in a spatial framework, though localization is not as sharp outside
foveal areas.
