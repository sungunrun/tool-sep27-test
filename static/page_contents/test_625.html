In this chapter, I discuss three further capacities that are auxiliaries of perception: learning,
anticipation, and imagining. I center on perceptual-level species of the generic capacities.

Perceptual Learning

Perceptual learning is sometimes studied as if it were a self-standing phenomenon. Some
perceptual learning resets perceptual processes directly. But quite a lot of perceptual learn-
ing depends on perceptual memory.

Psychologists who work on perceptual learning regard it as a sensory or perceptual phe-
nom enon, not a cognitive phenomenon. The specifically perceptual constraints on rele-
vant tasks and the locations of neural support motivate counting it perceptual. Since it is
specialized to perception, it is often not even generalized associative learning, which in
itself is not taken to be cognitive.978 So the practice—engaged in by some philosophers
and a very few psychologists—of citing learning, without qualification, as a cognitive
capacity is out of step with standard views in the sub-field. Being out of step has no con-
ceptual or experimental justification.
