Many discussions follow Fodor and Pylyshyn in this focus. However, MacPherson is not

I believe that focus on experience can be harmless, as long as it is contrasted with the
Fodor–Pylyshyn focus, and from my own. A focus on experience does have a baleful ten-
dency in philosophy to be associated with not thinking empirically about psychological
natural kinds. Much such discussion is off-hand. There is no advanced science of percep-
tual experience per se, or indeed of consciousness. Perceptual experience, colloquially
understood, is a hybrid of perceptual and cognitive (conceptual) elements. As I indicated,
MacPherson’s own defense of her claim of cognitive penetration of visual experience elides
crucial differences between perceptual and conceptual attributives, and between beliefs
and lower-level associations.1113

Pylyshyn writes, ‘Our subjective experience of the world fails to distinguish among the
various sources of this experience, whether they arise from the visual system or from our
beliefs’.1114 I say, indeed.

Psychological science routinely uses voluntary reports from subjects in experiments as
evidence for hypotheses about psychological structure. Such reports derive from the sub-
jects’ conscious states. The reports’ relation to explanatory models is complex. There are
many other sources of evidence—neural, non-verbal behavioral, force-choice tests, for
example. The consciousness involved in accessing experience is different from the phe-
nomenal consciousness that one accesses. Access often involves judgments. Some aspects
of experience do not.1115 It is reasonable to be interested in putative cognitive effects on

the early visual system. MacPherson’s main points are not much affected by these mistakes, or indeed by her
