Perceptual Imagining

I take perceptual imagining to include perceptual dreaming, perceptual day-dreaming,
and perceptual imagery whether endogenously controlled or not. I do not include images
in visual sensory memory that are immediate. natural residues of perception. (See
Chapter 16, the section Visual Sensory Memory.) Images may arise in imagination
from any memory, and may or may not be altered there. Imagining differs from memory
not necessarily in its contents, but in its representational function. Unlike memory, per-
ception, and anticipatory representation, imagining is non-committal. It does not realize
or fail to realize any representational function by being either inaccurate or accurate. An
imagining’s being inaccurate is not in itself a representational failure. When imagining
uses only perceptual attributives, it is perceptual imagining.

Dreaming in visual form is an exercise of the imagination that may have only practical
functions. Endogenously controlled visual imagining can have functions of play, represen-
tational expansion, or catharsis. There is evidence for imaginative functions, in both rest
and sleep, in some non-human animals, such as rats.1024

Some imagining refers to actual entities. Humans and probably apes and cats can dream
about their mothers. Much imagining has unapplied schematic contents or referential
applications that do not refer to any particular. Then properties are indicated, but no par-
ticulars are referred to.
