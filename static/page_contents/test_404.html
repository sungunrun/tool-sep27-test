A second failure in the argument lies in its illegitimate comparison of super-
blindsight, on one hand, with attempts to understand the linguistic demonstrative ref-
erences of another individual who gives no cues as to which thing she ‘means’ or
demonstratively refers to in thought, on the other hand. There is no mechanism or
competence that, lacking such cues, could enable one to track which face another indi-
vidual means. We do not read others’ minds except through perceived cues. Absent
such cues and absent a relevant competence and mechanism, getting right what another
person means is guessing. Campbell’s hearer has no route to track the other’s demon-
strative usage, or to replicate it in thought. Here attribution of guessing is plausible. But
this situation differs from both blindsight and super-blindsight. Blindseers and super-
blindseers have a known competence and approximately known mechanism for navi-
gating the world. Super-blindseers understand the demonstratives in their thoughts in
the only sense that is necessary for demonstrative reference. They are competent in
picking out objects through perception and through perception-based demonstrative
thought.

The goal of Campbell’s argument is to show that consciousness is necessary for blind-
seers’ use of demonstratives in thought. The example should have exhibited a case in which
demonstrative thought is absent and the only feature that can account for its absence is
absence of consciousness. In the thought experiment, not only is consciousness absent.
Also absent is any competence or mechanism for following and replicating the other per-
son’s demonstrative reference. This latter absence suffices to explain the hearer’s failure to
think demonstratively about what the speaker means. Campbell’s drawing his conclusion
from his example is a non sequitur.

Campbell frames the discussion as if it concerned linguistic understanding. The
relevant understanding is a perception-based intellectual competence to rely on per-
ception in contextually picking out objects in thought. The super-blindseer has a com-
petence to think perception-based thoughts. The thoughts are just as de re and
demonstrative-governed as the perceptions are. The perceptions are de re and involve
demonstrative-like referential applications. The perceptions pick out objects by
contextual-causal means, embedded in perceptual constancies. The super-blindseer is
competent in unconsciously picking out objects. Demonstrative-like referential appli-
cations in perceptual belief and perception-based thought are automatically formed
from counterpart applications in perception. A competence to consciously pick out
objects and consciously focus on them is not necessary for picking out objects de re in
perceptual belief.482

Cont FN482 Some of Campbell’s argument rests on the entirely armchair claim that a blindseer only perceives func-
tional properties, “affordances”: Campbell, Reference and Consciousness, 138–145. It is an open empirical question
whether any functional attributives are produced in the visual system (as distinguished from the actional sys-
tem), though I assume here and elsewhere that some are. (See Chapter 12.) Campbell’s claim is, however, flatly
incompatible with science. Vision science never takes perceptual attribution of functional properties to be
entirely independent of perceptual attribution of “categoreal” properties like shape. Shape attribution is certainly
computed first. One can perceive something as prey only by perceiving it as having attributes like specific shape,
size, and motion.
