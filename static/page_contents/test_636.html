636 Perceptual Learning, Anticipation, Imagining


Obviously, anticipatory representation occurs not only in simple motion tracking. It
occurs in tracking through occlusion. For longer than the briefest occlusions (longer than
200 ms), it is not possible to integrate consecutive visual samples into a spatio-temporally
continuous percept. Mammalian perception, and probably perception by birds and other
non-mammals, provides constancy of attributes such as body, body-shape, and motion
trajectory. Motion and particulars can be tracked through occlusion. Anticipation of pos-
ition of a moving object after an occlusion, by maintaining constancies for trajectory,
velocity, and acceleration, is widespread and well known. Details of storage and conditions
of anticipatory tracking when a moving object disappears have been studied in some depth.

For example, it takes a longer exposure to a pattern of motion to anticipate acceleration
accurately than it does to anticipate velocity.1010 As with anticipatory smooth pursuit of a
fully visible object, such pursuit of an occluded object is not purely a reflex or driven
purely by visual input and visual memory. Systems can adjust to task-related targets.1011

Anticipatory representation also occurs among lower mammals preparing for action.
Viewing delivery of food to an as yet unvisited position produces pre-activation of repre-
sentations that anticipate navigation toward that position. Evidence comes from activation
of hippocampal neurons that can be mapped to the position associated with future
reward.1012

I have emphasized the primitiveness of mechanisms in anticipatory representation that
tracks motion. They include efferent copy and condition mechanisms. Here these old
mechanisms embed representational states. Any system capable of perceptually represent-
ing motion can be expected to have incorporated one or both of these mechanisms. The
second, the more primitive one, is, of course, applicable in perceptually representing
almost any repetitive pattern. Some anticipatory and short-term-memory capacity can be
expected throughout animal perception.

It would be obviously mistaken to count these mechanisms cognitive in a sense that
entails supra-perceptual. They are nearly inevitable companions of perception. The mem-
ory mechanisms and conditional mechanisms that figure in simple forms of anticipatory
representation are less complex, less a result of impressive evolution, than formation pro-
cesses in perception itself. A divide between the perceptual and the cognitive should be a
significant joint among psychological capacities. No such joint divides perception from
perceptual memory or perceptual anticipation.

Perceptual memory and anticipatory perceptual-level representation do contrast with
perception in being more removed from representation of here and now. Representation’s
reach beyond functioning to respond to present stimulation is certainly a difference
between memory and anticipation, on one hand, and perception, on the other.
