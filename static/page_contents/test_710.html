Second, only beliefs and intentions whose content is a propositionalized version of the
perceptual-level attention command or motor command gives even the appearance of fall-
ing into a pattern that could be explained computationally. Selecting very specific beliefs
and intentions that “work” does not add to systematic understanding. The beliefs and
intention types would be selected only because they make computing a function easier.
Their content types are selected because they derive from the perceptual-level content
types. The perceptual-level types produce the computation whether or not they are caused
by an instance of the propositional-level types.

Third, really the point underlying the second: What is distinctive about the prop os-
ition al states—their place in a network of reason-providing and explanation-providing
inferences—plays no role in the extended computation. It is only by bracketing those
states’ inferential connections to other propositional states that one can allow them into a
workable computation. Then, their occurring in the computation contributes nothing to
understanding a system of computation.

Explanations that cite propositional-level causes do provide insight into causal connec-
tions between propositional states and the perceptual motor system. Such explanations do
not yield insight into the systematic computational pattern that results in production of
perceptual-level states. They just select elements of another system, held together by prop-
os ition al inference, that do, on some occasions, causally determine the inputs that the
computational system actually, systematically operates upon.

Psychological Systems and Psychological Kinds

I have used the terms ‘visual system’, ‘visuo-motor system’, ‘perceptual system’, ‘perceptual-
motor system’, leaving them open-ended. I think that they mark psychological natural
kinds. The kinds overlap and interlock. Can we reduce the open-endedness a bit?
