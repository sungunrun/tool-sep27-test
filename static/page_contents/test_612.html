Priming evinces contents in long-term memory that are not easily accessible to con-
sciousness. Individuals are faster and more accurate in identifying a seen object that they
have seen before, and in identifying an environment type of object or configuration of
objects that they have seen before. Evidence of not easily accessible long-term memory
also comes from forced-choice recognition and other sources, such as eye movement.
Priming and forced-choice effects can be specific to details that were once visually per-
ceived. The effects show up in performance over a year after initial perception.954
Unconsciously fed, long-term memories can remain intact despite serious deficits in long-
term conscious recall. Some of these memories may not be retrievable into consciousness.
They show their presence in affecting behavior.955

Long-term memory has been mistakenly viewed as a repository only of purely abstract,
even amodal or conceptual representations. It has been claimed that detailed images
deriving from perception do not survive in long-term memory. Some of these claims stem
from over-focus on accessible, on-demand conscious images. Image-based or template-
based retrieval in long-term memory facilitates search and categorization independently
of conceptual labeling of a searched-for item. The templates do not retain all the detail of
perception. They are, however, rich in detail.956

The mistaken view of long-term memory as always abstract has probably contributed to
the mistaken view that long-term memory is, without qualification, a higher, cognitive
competence—because of the half-baked empiricist idea that cognition is marked by
abstraction. In fact, long-term memory functions to retain both conceptual and percep-
tual representations. Neither need be abstract; both can be abstract. Both can also be richly
iconic. Retained conceptual representations can be iconic or not. There is evidence that
perceptual VLTM and perceptual WM share (iconic) format. There is no evidence that
this format always depends on propositional capacities. Retained perceptual contents are
always iconic, rich or not. Neither the representational contents of perceptual long-term
memories nor the operations that yield those contents support taking LTM, without quali-
fication, to be cognitive in a sense that would mark it as a supra-perceptual power.
