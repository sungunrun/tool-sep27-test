Perceptual representation of a color under one illumination is a different perceptual
capacity from perceptual representation of the color under another. Perceptual represen-
tation of shape in one orientation, with respect to the viewer, is a different capacity from
perceptual representation of the shape in another orientation. Since perceptual at tribu-
tives are perceptual capacities, perceptual attributives for a given color differ. Similarly, for
shape attributives.

There is further ground to think that perceptual attributives are what they are by virtue
not only of what they represent, but also of how they represent it. Neural evidence indi-
cates that the retinal information from which different perceptual attributives are com-
puted in representing a representatum under a significant variety of retinal stimuli is not
lost track of.224 This result should not be surprising. Introspectively, a perception as of a
given shape or color from one perspective has a different “look” from a perception as of
that attribute from another.

In sum, an account of the form of perceptual representation must distinguish percep-
tual attributives for a given representatum, where all them are linked in a perceptual
constancy.

A slightly more realistic way of marking differences among ways in which a given
attribute is indicated and attributed is as follows:

(that x1)

[(that x2)(that x3)

(brownr1(c)(x1), brownr1(i)(x2), cubicalp1(c)(x1), cubicalp1(i)(x3), bodyd1(i)(x1))].

The formula marks both one of various perceptual attributives for a given attribute
(cu bic al), indicated by the superscripts, and the unity among them, indicated by the words
(‘cubical’) that derives from their being linked. The attributive for body has a specific mode
of presentation deriving at least partly from the modes of presentation of the shape and
color attributives that triggered the attribution of body. If the color were illuminated dif-
ferently, or if the shape were at a different depth or orientation, the superscripts would
differ. If the body were presented with a different shape orientation or a different color, the
body attributive’s symbol would be superscripted differently. If the attributives are exer-
cised in a perceptual constancy for one attribute, the main word-like attributive symbol
(‘brown’, ‘cubical’, ‘body’) remains the same. A perceptual constancy is a network of these
attributives (attributive capacities) linked in indicating and attributing a given attribute.
