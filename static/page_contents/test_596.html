Evidence that VWM plays this role is varied and solid. For example, a concurrent repre-
sentational load in VWM interferes with the efficiency of searches, unless representations
of features of the search target repeat over many trials and need not be maintained in
VWM.894 Complex searches for multiple objects correlate well with establishing multiple
templates that guide attention to the objects.895 There is also neurological evidence.
Neurological correlates of maintenance of VWM are active through visual search. Higher
amplitudes of activity correlate with search success.896 Finally, the phenomenon of
memory-driven capture evinces VWM’s role in visual search. Participants are asked to
memorize a priming item, such as a red rectangle. The red rectangle might be either a
search target or a distractor in a subsequent search display. Targets and distractors are
easily discriminable. A target might even be a one-of-a-kind in the display, and thus sub-
ject to pop-out. Nevertheless, even if the prime in working memory is a distractor, not a
target, it normally affects the latency and direction of eye movement.897 The memory-
driven capture effect evinces a close relation between VWM and direction of attention in
visual search. Normally, outside the laboratory, when what is held in working memory
specifies a target, memory-driven capture aids direction of attention to searched-for
targets.

Similar evidence supports taking VWM to support extended tracking of moving
objects, including multiple-object tracking, a close analog of visual search.898

In all these cases, one should distinguish VWM, a system for retention, from attention.
The retention and the initiations of attention have been shown to be distinct.899 There is
evidence that dissociates retention from functions of executive control, such as suppres-
sion of distractors.900

VWM functions not only to retain information but also to guide action. VWM figures in
navigation—in tracking objects, object location, and object relations, as an individual
moves.901
